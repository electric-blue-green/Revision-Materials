B. RRS2 - Rocks
1. Rocks belong to one of three groups. Their formation is linked by the rock cycle
2. The 3 Groups
a) Igneous Rocks
(1) Formed by cooling of molten magma either above (extrusive) or under (intrusive) the ground.
(2) Composed of interlocked crystals
(3) Very tough, and resistant to erosion
(4) Ex - Basalt (ext) and Granite (int)
(5) Intrusive/Extrusive
i) Intrusive rocks are formed underground, and take a long time to cool, and so have larger crystals
ii) Extrusive rocks are formed on the surface, so cool quickly, and have smaller crystals. Obsidian is an igneous rock formed instantly, with no crystals
b) Sedimentary Rocks
(1) Formed by the compaction and cementation of sediments, deposited in the sea. This includes organic material and precipitate.
(2) Found in distinct layers called Beds
(3) Often contain fossils
(4) Although some are hard (limestone) most are the softest typed or rock
(5) Ex - Limestone, shale
c) Metamorpic
(1) Formed by the alteration of existing Igneous or Sedimentary rock my heat and/or pressure
(2) Most show layers (NOT BEDS) called cleavage (slate) and banding
(3) Often quite tough and resistant to erosion
(4) Ex - Slate, Gneiss, Schist
3. The Rock Cycle
a) Rocks are constantly being recycled and reformed
b) For example, igneous rock can be broken down by weathering, forming precipitate in the sea, and settling as sediment, forming Sedimentary rock. This can then be uplifted to form a mountain range, and is put under enormous pressure and forms metamorphic rock. Some of this then may melt into magma, and form another igneous rock.
C. RRS3 - Weathering
1. Rocks are susceptible to weathering. The type of weathering that is most effective is determined by the composition of the rock and the climate.
2. The 3 Types of Weathering
a) Mechanical
(1) AKA Physical weathering
(2) Involves the disintegration of rocks, with no chemical change. Often results in piles of angular rock
(3) Ex - Exfoliation
i) ‘Onion skin weathering’
ii) Occurs in areas with large temperature fluctuations like deserts. In heat change, only the outer layers heat up, causing them to expand, and contract at night.
iii) Repeated cycles of expanding/contracting cause the outer layers to fall away.
iv) Water is important, as it softens the rock to allow it to expand, and makes it more vulnerable to flaking away.
(4) Ex - Freeze-thaw
i) Water gets into cracks in the rock during the day
ii) At night temperature falls below 0C so the water freezes and expands exerting pressure on the surrounding rock
iii) The next day the temperature rises above 0C so the ice melts
iv) Daily repetition of this cycle over time will weaken rocks and fragments will fall away
b) Chemical
(1) A chemical change occurs
(2) Rainwater is slightly acidic, so will slowly dissolve some rocks.
(3) Particles that do not get affected normally form a clay deposit
(4) Ex - Solution: Some rocks can dissolve in rainwater like sugar dissolves in tea. Rock salt is left behind when a salt lake dries up, and deposits the salt behind in solid form. These can be extracted an used as road salt.
http://gcsealevelrevision.blogspot.co.uk/2012/04/geography-rocks-resources-and-scenery.html #Chemical Wethering
(5) Ex - Carbonation: Similar to solution, and affects Calcium Carbonate rocks (CaCO3) like limestone and chalk. During this, rainwater reacts with CO2 to make weak carbonic acid in the clouds. This reacts with the CaCO3 to form calcium bicarbonate that dissolves.
c) Biological
(1) This involves the action of flora and fauna.
(2) Roots are effective at growing and expanding rocks
(3) Animals can dig through weak rocks.
D. RRS4 - Rock Landforms
1. Different rocks create contrasting landforms and landscapes - study granite, chalk and clay, and Carboniferous limestone
2. Granite Formation and Distribution
a) Granite is an intrusive igneous rock that was formed deep underground millions of years ago
b) Following uplift and erosion of the rocks above it, it became exposed to the surface
c) The SW if England all the different areas of exposed granite are actually all the same Batholith. The irregular surface it gradually being exposed
d) One day, SW England will be entirely granite
e) The distribution in the UK is party, and apart from exposures in SW England, it is only found in NW England, Scotland, and Ireland
3. Characteristics of Granite
a) Granite is very tough and resistant to erosion
b) This is why it creates many upland areas such as Dartmoor and Bodmin in SW England
c) There are 3 main minerals in granite - Grey quartz, black mica, and pink feldspar. Feldspar is vulnerable to chemical weathering and will decay into white clay called china or kaolin.
d) Granite contains cracks or joints. Vertical joints form when granite could and contracts.
e) Horizontal joints from from pressure release when overlaying rocks are removed. The rock expanded as pressure was released, and cracked in parallel to the ground.
f) These cracks make it vulnerable to mechanical weathering like Freeze-Thaw.
g) Despite these joints, it is impermeable, and does not allow water to filter through. This means granite areas are often marshy and have rivers.
4. Granite Landforms
a) The most distinctive landform are isolated outcrops called tors.
b) These are found on the top of hills and look liked piles of dumped rocks. They are however part of the solid geology, and are embedded in the hill

c) The accepted theory for their formation was proposed in 1955 by Linton
d) The theory suggests that the spacing of vertical joints varied, and changed how effective weathering was.
e) The rock underground and in a warmer and wetter climate had closer joints withered faster than areas with widely spaced joints.
f) As the granite was exposed in the Ice Age, erosion and mass movement removed the broken up granite, leaving the unweathered jointed granite to form a tor.
g) The tor has continued to be weathered since.
5. Chalk and Clay Landforms
a) Chalk is made of CaCO3, and is porous so allows water to filter through it.
b) This means surface streams are only visible when the rock is saturated, as water flows through the rock.
c) Dry valleys are valleys formed by fast flowing streams, but now the water does not flow on the surface.
d) Where permeable meets impermeable, springs are formed as the water is forced to the surface
e) Chalk forms gentle hills inland, and cliffs on the sea.
f) Escarpments are formed when there are two rock types. The water table forms above the chalk but below the surface, and slopes steeply to one side where there is clay, forming a stream. The other side is a gentle slope. These are also good for aquifying water

6. Limestone formations
a) Yorkshire Dales is the largest formation of limestone
b) Limestone is all CaCO3m and slowly dissolves in carbonic acid. It is structured like building blocks with joints (V) and bedding planes (H)
c) Landforms include swallow/sink holes where acid has widened a joint, and the stream disappears underground
d) Limestone pavements are formed where limestone has been exposed, and the joints between the blocks of tock widens to form grikes. These blocks are called clints. Example - Malham Cove
e) Dry Valleys - same as chalk
f) Gorges - the roof on an underground cave system collapses, and leaves a stream flowing at the base of a narrow valley
g) Caves - found when a stream going down a swallow hole has dissolved rock underground. Deposits of limestone hand from the ceiling in stalactites, and rise from the floor as stalagmites (T - hanging from ceiling, M - rising from floor)
E. RRS5 - Resources
1. Granite, chalk and clay, and Carboniferous limestone provide resources to extract, land to farm, and unique scenery for tourism
2. Chalk Areas
a) Pastoral farming - Mainly sheep, racehorses and some cows are farmed. Soils are too thin to allow much arable farming.
b) Settlements Where chalk meets clay, spring-line settlements can be seen, as springs are a useful resource. Due to the good access to London, the South Downs are popular as a location for commuters and retired people.
c) Racehorse training - For example, stud farms and stables are common at Epsom in Surrey.
d) Quarrying - Chalk with flints is a strong building material used in cement manufacture.
3. Granite Areas
a) Extraction of china clay - Kaolin, also known as china clay, is a product of hydrolysis and is used in ceramics and paper-making, and is formed from the decay of Feldspar. The industry is very important for the local economy and employment.
b) Quarrying - Granite blocks were used for dry-stone walling, buildings and road stone. Many quarries are now abandoned, but granite is still used.
c) Tourism - Dartmoor is a National Park, popular for walking, camping and pony trekking. Over 10 million people visit the area each year.
d) Farming - The soils are poor and acidic, and frequently used for sheep farming rather than growing crops.
e) Mining - Copper and tin mining used to be common in the area. The metals were found in thin veins within the granite.
4. Limestone Areas
a) Quarrying - Limestone is quarried in the Yorkshire Dales and is very important for the local economy. Limestone is used for building, cement and fertiliser. Limestone is also used in the steel industry, eg Castle Bolton quarry in Wensleydale, North Yorkshire.
b) Tourism - Tourists visit the area for walking, camping, climbing, pot-holing, caving, educational visits and for the distinctive scenery. Over 8 million visitors provide employment and an important boost to the local economy, where farming has become less profitable.
c) Farming - Sheep farming is common, as the soils are thin and unsuitable for any other type of farming. Some farmers diversify into camping and bed and breakfast accommodation.

B. IOL2 - Glacial Budget
1. The amount of ice depends on the glacial budget. This has seen a loss since 1950, with seasonal fluctuations due to seasonal temperatures
2. Glacial Budgets
a) This is the balance between input and output of a specific glacier
b) As a glacier slopes down a mountain, the zone of Accumulation (inputs) is found near the top. This is where snow and rain add to the glaciers mass.
c) The areas further down is the zone of Ablation (outputs) where meltwater, evaporation, and moraine deposit takes from the mass of the glacier
d) Between these is where the processes of Plucking, abrasion, and transportation take place.
e) A glacier ALWAYS moves very slowly down a valley. When it appears to be going back up the valley, it is simply the end of the frozen glacier retreating.
f) In physical terms, as the temperature increases, the Ablation zone moves higher and higher up, so more area is loosing ice, and so the from retreats, depositing its moraine. As temperature goes down, the Ablation zone moves down, so more and more ice stays frozen

3. Balance
a) Seasonally, the balance shits. In summer, there is higher loss/output, so more ablation. In winter there is more gain/input, so more accumulation. This should average out.
b) When there is an AVERAGE change in temperature, the glacier starts retreating or advancing significantly. As average temperatures over the year rise, the glacier retreats. This is part of a larger system, either the natural ice age cycle, or man made global warming. If there is a decrease in GATemps (such as leading up to an ice age) glacier advance
C. IOL3 - Glacial Processes
1. Ice is a powerful force in shaping the land as a result of weathering, erosion, transportation and deposition
2. Erosional Processes
a) Abrasion
(1) Where moraine in a glacier acts like sandpaper on the rock below, the sharp edges cutting in to the rock
b) Plucking
(1) Where the ice freezes onto a rock, and as the ice moves, plucks the rock free
(2) This rock becomes moraine
c) Freeze Thaw
(1) When water enters cracks in rocks, it can freeze and expand
(2) This breaks the rock apart
3. Depositional Processe
a) Bolldozing
(1) Where terminal moraine is pushed further by an advancing glacier
b) Deposition
(1) Where a glacier runs out of energy, or enchants an obsiace
D. IOL4 - Glacial Landforms
1. Distinctive landforms result from different processes
2. Erosional Landforms
a) Corries
(1) Carries face N/NE
(2) Snow falls and compacts into ice in a small hole
(3) As is gradually slips, it becomes a glacier.
(4) The rotational movement caused by lubrication plucks rocks and moves them to the rock lip
(5) The back and bottom are eroded, making it deeper, with a combination of plucking, abrasion and depositional erosion
(6) The melting allows more rotation, and pronounces the rock lip
(7) The slippage created a Bergchrund crevasse
b) Glacial Troughs
(1) These are U shaped glacial valleys created by a glacier moving through an existing V shaped valley created by a river
(2) A glacier can’t move and meander, so it uses its immense weight to plough through the rock, and create a U shaped basin
(3) This created hanging valleys, which are valleys that run at 90 degrees to the main valley, and get cut off half way up the side of the valley by the glacier
(4) It also truncates interlocking spurs into truncated spurs
c) Arêtes and peaks
(1) An arête is a knife edge peak created by two meeting carries back to back.
(2) As the two backs meet, they create a thin ridge
(3) Examples include Snowdon

(4) If three or more carries meet, they create a pyramidal peak, a very high pyramid shaped peak
(5) Examples include the Matterhorn and Mount Everest

3. Depositional Landforms
a) Erratics
(1) All glacial deposits remain unsorted unlike rivers and streams
(2) The extremes of this can be seen as erratics, where large rocks and boulders are found on their own
(3) These are unusual shapes, and are very large, and are an uncommon rock type to their location
b) Drumlins
(1) These are elongated, half-egg-shaped hills of glacial deposit.
(2) They can me 1km long and 500m wide. A group of them s called a basket of eggs
(3) These are made out of debris that stopped moving when there was an obstacle
(4) The long axis indicated the direction of the glacier, but there is disagreement to exactly how they are formed
(5) Slow slope = lee slope. Steep slope = stress end

c) Moraine Types
(1) Terminal
i) Found at the terminus (end point) reached by the glacier
(2) Lateral
i) Found deposited along the side of the glacier
(3) Medial
i) Found at the junction and seam between two glaciers
(4) Gorund
i) Disorganised piles found on the ground

III. Unit 1B: The Coastal Zone
A. TCZ1 - Coastal Shaping
1. The coast is shaped by weathering, mass movement, erosion, transportation and deposition
2. Weathering Processes
a) Mechanical
(1) Mechanical uses kinetic energy to break apart rocks
(2) It uses the movement of other particles to erode rock
(3) There are no major molecular changes
b) Chemical
(1) Chemical uses chemical energy to break down rocks
(2) This changes the molecular structure
3. Mass Movement
a) Rockfall
(1) Rapid free fall from a steep cliff
(2) Bare jointed rock is vulnerable to breaking
(3) Loosened by gravity and freeze thaw
(4) Scree slope is formed
b) Mudflow
(1) Slopes over 10 degrees
(2) Rapid sand or soil movement after rain
(3) Caused by a lack of vegetation
c) Landslip
(1) Occasional rapid movement
(2) Fall on a concave plane after rainfall
(3) Makes slumps likely
d) Soil Creep
(1) Very slow movement of soil
(2) Moves when soil expands and contracts
4. Wave Types
a) Constructive
(1) Low amplitude
(2) Long wavelength
(3) Strong swash
(4) Weak back wash
(5) Preferable to surfers
b) Destructive
(1) High amplitude
(2) Short wavelength
(3) Weak swash
(4) Strong backwash
(5) Broken by local winds
5. Erosion Processes
a) Hydraulic
(1) The sheer force of water eroding the rock
b) Abrasion
(1) Small sand particles acting as sandpaper against rock
c) Attrition
(1) Similar sized rocks hitting together and breaking apart
d) Solution
(1) When rocks are dissolved by chemicals
6. Transportation Processes
a) Longshore Drift
(1) When waves come into the shore at any angle, they always deflect at 90 degrees
(2) This means particles can be transported down the shore
(3) Rocks found downwards on the LSD are smaller and smoother due to attrition and abrasion
b) Traction
(1) When large boulders roll across the ocean floor
(2) Remember: Tractor wheels
c) Saltation
(1) When small boulders bounce across the ocean in waves
(2) Remember: Salt shaker bouncing on the table
d) Suspension
(1) When small rock particles are being carried in the sea, but not dissolved
e) Solution
(1) When rock dissolves into the sea and is transported
B. TCZ2 - Land Forms
1. Erosional Landforms
a) Headlands
(1) Headlands are formed when a sea attacks a coast with alternating bands of hard and soft rock
(2) As the soft rock erodes quickly, it leaves a jut of hard rock forming a headland
(3) The soft rock forms a bay
b) Cliffs
(1) Formed by a combination of erosion and weathering
(2) Soft rock erodes easily to form slopes and hills
(3) Hard rock erodes slowly forming steep cliffs
c) Wave cut platforms
(1) New rock face exposed to the sea
(2) Face is exposed to hydraulic action mainly at high and low tide points
(3) Wave cut notch is formed at sea level
(4) Cliff retreats as the section above the notch falls
(5) Platform remains at low tide level
d) Caves, arches, and stacks
(1) First, a large crack opens in a cliff face by hydraulic action
(2) Second, the crack grows into a cave by hydraulic and abrasive action
(3) Third, the cave slowly becomes larger
(4) Fourth, the cave breaks through to the other side and forms a natural arch
(5) Fifth, the top of the arch is eroded and colappses in
(6) Sixth, a tall rock stack is left
(7) Finally, the stack is eroded into a stump
2. Depositional Landforms
a) Beaches
(1) Beaches are accumulations of sand and shingle
(2) Beaches can be straight or curved depending on formation and the refraction of refraction of waves
(3) Constructive waves build up beaches
b) Spits
(1) Spits are built up when Longshore drift carries sand along a beach
i) Prevailing winds bring in sand at an angle
ii) Zig-zag down the beach
(2) Spit is formed when the sand runs out of beach
(3) Creates shelters water behind - salt flats
(4) Curved as wind changes direction

E. TCZ5 - Coastal Management
1. There is discussion about how the coast should be managed.
There is debate about the costs and benefits of “hard” and “soft” engineering
2. Hard Engineering
a) Sea Walls
(1) Tall concrete wall built on the edge of the coastline
(2) Advantages: Protects the base of cliffs, land and buildings against erosion.
Can prevent coastal flooding in some areas.
(3) Disadvantages: Expensive to build. Curved sea walls reflect the energy of the
waves back to the sea. This means that the waves remain powerful. Over time
the wall may begin to erode. The cost of maintenance is high.
b) Groynes
(1) A wooden barrier built at right angles to the beach.
(2) Advantages: Prevents the movement of beach material along the coast by longshore drift.
Allows the build up of a beach. Beaches are a natural defence against erosion and an attraction for tourists.
(3) Disadvantages: Can be seen as unattractive. Costly to build and maintain.
c) Rock armour or boulder barriers
(1) Advantages: Absorb the energy of waves. Allows the build up of a beach.
(2) Disadvantages: Can be expensive to obtain and transport the boulders.
3. Soft Engineering
a) Beach Management
(1) This replaces beach or cliff material that has been removed by erosion
or longshore drift.
(2) The main advantage is that beaches are a natural defence against erosion
and coastal flooding. Beaches also attract tourists.
(3) It is a relatively inexpensive option but requires constant maintenance to replace
the beach material as it is washed away.
b) Managed Retreat
(1) Areas of the coast are allowed to erode and flood naturally. Usually this will be areas
considered to be of low value - eg places not being used for housing or farmland.
(2) The advantages are that it encourages the development of beaches (a natural defence)
and salt marshes (important for the environment) and cost is low.
(3) Managed retreat is a cheap option, but people will need to be compensated for loss of
buildings and farmland.

1. Urbanisation is a global phenomenon
2. Definition
a) Urbanisation is the increasing percentage of the population living in urban areas.
b) Rapid urbanisation is happening in LEDCs because if rural-urban migration
3. Causes
a) Very few amenities in rural areas (Push)
b) Education and health is far better in the cities (Pull)
c) Better jobs in urban areas (Pull)
d) Better quality of life in urban areas (Pull)
B. CUE2 - Urban Areas
1. Urban areas have a variety of functions and land uses
2. Sections of an Urban Area
a) Central Business District
(1) Land Uses
i) Major Shops
ii) Pedestrianised areas
iii) Civic buildings
iv) Transport hubs/terminals
v) Few houses
(2) Models
i) Core-frame Model

(a) This also follows the Burgess model
(b) It encompasses the whole CBD
(c) The department stores and shops are found in the Core
(d) The smaller shops, entertainment and offices are found in the outer core/inner frame
(e) The social services, manufacturing and education are found in the frame
b) Inner City
(1) Terraced houses close to workspaces
(2) Very run down
(3) Some areas abandoned
c) Suburb
(1) Residential area
(2) Semi-detached
(3) Less traffic
(4) Planned roads
(5) Cheaper land
(6) New development on the outer fringe
d) Urban/Rural Fringe
(1) Agriculture
(2) Mixed land use
(3) Strict planning laws
(4) Green belt
i) No development
ii) Prevents uncontrolled urban expansion
3. Models of an Urban Area
a) Burgess Model

(1) Areas are organised in rings
(2) Land values are highest at the centre
(3) This leads to high-rise high-density areas
(4) This model is very old
(5) New working/housing trends have emerged and do not follow this model
b) Hoyt Model

(1) This is based on the Burgess model, but has land sectors.
(2) Some ones, like the factory zone, radiate out of the CBD
(3) These follow transport lines

D. CUE4 - Squatter Settlements CS
1. Rapid Urbanisation has led to the development of squatter settlements and an informal sector of the economy CASE STUDY: KIBERA
2. Squatter Settlements
a) Squatter settlements are found on the outside of cities in less developed countries
b) These are rapidly expanding because many people are migrating from villages and do not have skills to find proper work.
c) When they enter the city, these settlements are they first thing they meet, so they become part of the community.
d) Because these people are unskilled, they are forced to stay in the slums.
e) Many of these people create their own employment. This is called the informal sector, and these are not officially recorded
f) These settlements lack facilities, like clean water, electricity and transport.
g) Disease can also spread very fast as it is so densely populated.
3. Improving Squatter Settlements
a) Self help schemes
(1) The government provides materials so they can build there own homes.
(2) The money saved due to about can be used for providing utilities
b) Local authority schemes
(1) Funded by the local government
(2) Provides temporary accomodation
c) Site and Service
(1) Residents pay a small rent for a site with money they are assisted to earn, and borrow to get materials.
(2) The rent provides mainly for utilities
4. Case Study - Kibera
a) Where
(1) Kibera is just to the south of Nairobi, the capital city of Kenya
(2) Kenya is on the east coast of Africa, below Ethiopia
b) Statistics
(1) There are about 2.5 million people living in 200 slum sites across Nairobi
(2) Squatters represent 60% of the population, but take up 6% of the land
(3) Kibera has 250,000 population
c) Economic Issues
(1) Very few people have jobs in the slums
(2) Work is very scarce and in high demand
(3) Most jobs are in the informal sector and are low paying
d) Environmental issues
(1) There is no clean water supply, leading to waterborne diseases
(2) Open sewers lead to even more spread of disease like cholera, and pollution
(3) The settlement is close to a river, causing regular floods
e) Social Issues
(1) HIV and AIDS is a massive issue causing many deaths
(2) Many children do not get an education, and so can’t work

V. Unit 2B: The Development Gap
A. TDG1 - Contrasts in Development and Division
1. Contrasts in development means that the world can be divided up in many ways
2. Measurement Indexes
a) GDP
(1) Economic
(2) Gross National Product
(3) Measure of wealth but does not account for some business tax
(4) A broad measure of a nation's total economic activity. GNP is the value of all finished goods and services produced in a country in one year by its nationals.
b) GNI per Capita
(1) Economic
(2) Gross National Income
(3) Measure of countries wealth
(4) The total domestic and foreign output claimed by residents of a country
(5) Consists of gross domestic product (GDP) plus factor incomes earned by foreign residents, minus income earned in the domestic economy by nonresidents
(6) Comparing this and GDP shows whether a nation's resources are put to capital creation or declining toward abroad
c) HDI
(1) Social
(2) Human Development Index
(3) Takes into account Long and Healthy Life (Life expectancy at birth), Knowledge (Education Index/Literacy Rates), Standard of Living (GNI Index)
(4) Factor of 0-1
(5) Norway consistently top at ~0.944
d) Birth rates
(1) Social
(2) Number of Live Births per 1,000 of the population per year
(3) Currently at 19 worldwide
e) Death Rates
(1) Social
(2) Number of Deaths per 1,000
(3) Currently at 8 worldwide
f) Infant Mortality
(1) Social
(2) The number of infants that die under 12 months per 1,000 live babies born

C. TDG3 - International Efforts
1. The reduction of global inequalities will require international efforts
2. World Trade Imbalance
a) LEDCs
(1) Farmers are not subsidised. They can’t compete with cheap surplus products from MEDCs
(2) Many poor countries rely on primary industry. They often have to import secondary manufactured goods from MEDCs
(3) Poor countries have to compete for lower prices for raw materials, fluxuating prices
(4) LEDCs are rarely part of blocs to protect prices
(5) LEDCs export primary and import secondary so run a TRADE DEFICIT
b) MEDCs
(1) Farmers are subsidised for crops: they sell surplus cheaply
(2) Most manufacturing happens in MEDCs so they export secondary products
(3) Quotas and tariffs restrict imports to protect industries
(4) MEDCs are often part of blocs (EU) that help trade
c) Balancing It Out
(1) Debt Abolition
i) Richer countries can agree to cut debt of some poorer countries so they can invest in development
(2) Conservation Swap
i) The debt some countries owe can be swapped for carrying out conservation projects
(3) Trading Groups
i) Fair trade
ii) Countries can join blocs to negotiate higher prices and set fair prices
iii) They are working together, not in competition so less likely to undercut
3. Aid
a) Aid is the transfer of resources from MEDC/s to LEDC/s
b) Advantages
(1) Provides Jobs
(2) Emergency aid saves lives
(3) Long term aid can improve living, jobs
(4) New infrastructure can be provided (schools, hospitals, roads)
(5) Tied aid boosts export and jobs in MEDCs
(6) Aid can open markets for goods in MEDCs
c) Disadvantages
(1) Aid is frequently in the form of a loan and LEDCs sink further into debt
(2) Food aid may cause local prices to fall and put farmers out of buisness
(3) Tied aid may force LEDCs to buy inappropriate technology
(4) Corrupt officials may pocket aid
(5) Large scale projects may damage environment and increase national debt
(6) Funding may not be available to keep schools and hospitals running
(7) Well paid jobs do not go to locals
(8) May depend on aid
d) Types
(1) Bilateral
i) Aid given direct from one gov to another
ii) Money, training, teaching, food, or other supplies
iii) Can be Tied Aid where there are conditions attached
iv) In 1994, UK have £134mn to Malaysia for the Peragu dam in return for a weapons deal worth £1.2bn
(2) Multilateral
i) Aid which comes from multiple govs or orgs
ii) Normally arranged by international orgs like World Bank or UN
iii) The World Bank gave £450mn for the Aswan High dam in Egypt but has now silted up fishing grounds in the Med
(3) Non-Governmental
i) Orgs like Oxfam or Save the Children run projects, many on a small scale
ii) These NGO’s raise their money from donations
iii) Pump Aid has provided 4,000 elephant pumps since 1998 and provided clean water to 1mn people
(4) Short Term
i) This is for immediate relief in emergencies such a famine, earthquakes, floods and droughts.
ii) This includes money, food, blankets, tents, and medical supplies
(5) Long Term
i) This is for economic and social development
ii) Its purpose is to improve the quality of life for poor people living in LEDCs

2. Tourism is the worlds largest income earner and was worth $500bn in 2007.
3. Tourism has become a popular global activity, and in 2008 there were 922 million international arrivals at airports.
4. Reasons for the increase in tourism
a) Safer to fly
b) Cheaper
(1) Ryanair and Easyjet have brought the prices down and increased volume
(2) Very wide range of travel options
c) Better transport infrastructure
d) Lifestyle choices
(1) More people work desk jobs and are wealthy
(2) This means they get holiday entitlement
e) More demand
f) Personal challenge or competition
g) More disposable income
(1) Partly because of salary rise, partly because of the fall in cost of essentials.
5. Economic Importance
a) Tourism generates a huge amount of wealth for countries, and generates jobs in countries at every stage of development
b) Many countries with small GDP’s rely heavily on tourism, with most Caribbean countries getting at least half their GDP from tourists from Europe, USA and China
c) MEDCs like USA, Spain and France all have huge tourism, but the percentage of their GDP from tourism is fairly low
d) These MEDCs have good infrastructure and accommodation, and are politically safe places
B. TOR2 - Management Strategies
1. Effective management strategies are the key to the continuing prosperity of tourist areas in the UK
2. Tourism in the UK
a) The UK is a very popular destination as it has a wide range of activities.
b) The UK also has very good infrastructure, such as Heathrow, the Channel Tunnel, and extensive road and rail networks
c) Tourism accounts for 8% of the GDP, and roughly 1 in 20 (1.3mn) work in jobs related to tourism
d) Tourism promotes UK’s image around the world, with Heritage Tourism being very important.
3. Factors that affect visitor numbers
a) Exchange rates - if the visitor’s currency is strong, they will be persuaded to come and spend more, but if the Pound is stronger, then they will spend less
b) Security and Terrorism - Terrorist incidents will reduce tourist numbers (July 2007 bombings, March 2017 stabbings)
c) Global Economy - if the economy is doing well, people will have more money and will be more likely to travel abroad; the UK would be a beneficiary of this.
4. Butler Model
a) The butler model is used to model the life cycle of a tourist areas, and shows expected tourist number changes.
b) Exploration - Low numbers, a few tourists decide to visit
c) Involvement - tourists become involved in the community and expand it further
d) Development - mass development begins, with facilities and infrastructure
e) Consolidation - stagnation begins, and the site is an established tourist area
f) Stagnation - tourist numbers reach a plateau
g) Either: rejuvenation - the area is rejuvenated and invested in, so numbers grow
h) Or: decline - nothing is invested and the area declines
