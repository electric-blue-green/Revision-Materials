A. Topic 1 - Materials
1. Topic 1.1 - Woods
a) Oak Wood

(1) Class: Deciduous Hardwood
(2) Pro: Strong, heavy and durable
(3) Con: Corrodes Iron/Steel with tannic acid
(4) $: Expensive
(5) Uses: High class furniture, garden furniture, tables
b) Mahogany Wood

(1) Class: Deciduous Hardwood
(2) Pro: Strong, medium weight, easy to work
(3) Con: Interlocking grain, often warps
(4) $: Expensive
(5) Uses: Furniture, musical instruments, carvings
c) Beech Wood

(1) Class: Deciduous Hardwood
(2) Pro: Close grained, hard tough and strong, odourless
(3) Con: Prone to warping
(4) $: Medium
(5) Uses: Toys, veneers, cutting boards, tool heads
d) Ash Wood

(1) Class: Deciduous Hardwood
(2) Pro: Tough, flexible, elastic, durable outdoors, shock abrosbant
(3) Con: Very flexible
(4) $: Cheap
(5) Uses: tools, ladders, sport equipment
e) Scotts Pine Wood

(1) Class: Coniferous Softwood
(2) Pro: Straight grained, easy to work, storng
(3) Cons: Knotty, often splinters
(4) $: Cheap
(5) Uses: Construction, joint, outdoor with a varnish
f) Ply

(1) Odd number of veneers glued
(2) Grain at 90 degrees
(3) Not prone to normal defects, available in large sheets
(4) Used in boat hulls (marine ply) and furniture
g) LDFb (Chip/Particle board)

(1) Very weak
(2) Strong in compression
(3) Used in cheap furniture
h) MDFb

(1) Heavy, easy to cut
(2) Not suitable outdoors
(3) Large sheet sizes
(4) Used for doors and furniture (CNC)
i) HDFb (Hardboard)

(1) Wow pulp compressed
(2) One side smooth, one side textured
(3) No real strength, not suitable outdoors
(4) Some carpeting, backs of furniture

2. Topic 3.2 - Material Processes and Forming
a) Sand casting
(1) Aluminium ingots are prepares my melting them in a furnace. Gas blows through the furnace, dispersing heat through the crucible.
(2) Flux is added to prevent oxidation, as well as a degassing tablet to remove bass bubbles
(3) The mould is made by adding casting sand to a model to a flat level. This layer dries, and spruce pins are added to provide holes to pour the aluminium in. The model is then removed and the mould prepared
(4) When the aluminium reaches 650 degrees, the slag is scraped off and the aluminium is poured into the mould
b) Slip casting
(1) This is reusable and used mainly used with pewter, silver, or aluminium
(2) Two halves of a mould make up the pattern, with a sprue to pour the metal in
(3) They are attached with wire, and the metal poured it. Once it has set, the halves are separated and reused.
c) Injection moulding
(1) Granules of plastic are fed into a hopper and heated to melt
(2) An Archimedes screw injects them into the mould
(3) It then cools into the shape of the mould
d) Extrusion
(1) Similar to injection moulding, but the granules are forced thorough a die to form a continuous shape
e) Blow moulding
(1) The process is similar to injection, but air is forces in as well
(2) This forms a cavity, and are used to produce plastic bottles
f) Compression moulding
(1) The mould is heated up, and squeezed onto a slug of material
(2) This pushes the material into shape
g) Line bending
(1) For acrylic, a heated filament is held under the line to be bent
(2) This weakens the plastic so it can be bent around the line
h) Vacuum forming
(1) A mould is placed with a plastic sheet over it
(2) The sheet is heated, and softnes
(3) The mould is moved upwards, and the air taken out from under the mould. This pulls the sheet around the mould
3. Topic 3.3 - Joining methods
a) Metals
(1) Cold riveting
i) Both sides need access
ii) A domed holder is placed in a vice, and the rivet placed on it
iii) The two sheets are placed over that, and the exposed end hit with a hammer
(2) Pop riveting
i) Only one side needs access
ii) The rivet is placed in the rivet pliers, and then passed
iii) The handles are then squeezed together to pull the pin through the rivet
iv) The pin then snaps off
(3) Tapping
i) This cuts a female screw thread
ii) Three taps are used - plug tap, second tap, and taper tap, using the taper first
(4) Threading
i) Forms a male thread. A die is placed in a wrench, and then screwed down the screw
ii) The screw size can be changed by squeezing the metal die
iii) A thread that is not parallel is called a drunken thread
(5) Silver Soldering
i) Spelter solder is made from silver, copper, and zinc
ii) Flux is applied and heated with a blowtorch, and then the spelter applied
(6) Brazing
i) Similar to soldering, and used on bike frames.
ii) Steel heated to 875C, and brazing rod added to melt
(7) Welding
i) Oxo-Acetylene is the most commonly used
ii) In welding, the filler rod is the same as the material being welded
iii) This requires a mask and ventilation
b) Woods
(1) Dovetail
i) Complex joint
Strong and resistant to forces
Dovetail prevents collapsing in carcass assemblies
(2) Dowel
i) Temporary, circular pins
Used in flat pack furniture
Not very strong alone
(3) Cross halving
i) Simple and easy to cut with a router
Used in divider sections of carcasses
Used in house building with beams
(4) Lap
i) Cut with a rebate plane or routed
Used to conceal bases of carcasses
(5) Bridle
i) Easy to cut
Used in non-weight-bearing frames
(6) Housing joint
i) Slot routed into wood
Routed or chiselled
Used to make shelf recesses

a) Varnish

Heatproof and Waterproof

Time consuming
b) Wax polish
(1) Dull gloss shine
Made from beeswax dissolved in turps to form a paste
Applied with a cloth
Wood needs to be sealed beforehand
Shows natural grain
c) Stain
(1) Enhances the natural grain colour
Decorative
Water, oil or spirit based
Long lasting
d) Paint
(1) Woods:
Sealed to prevent resin lean
Sanded down
Top and undercoat
Oil or polyurethane based
Metals:
Metals have to be prepared before painting
Red oxide prevents rusting

(1) Suitable for most metals
Used for hanging baskets, brackets etc

Dipped into plastic granules and taken out

Cheap metal can have an outer coating
Carried out by electrolysis
Expensive and takes time
